## Delivery notes for the clone build

- Pace: 140–150 wpm. This is a boardroom pitch, not a TikTok. Slower than the Content Engine VSL. Land every number.
- `(pause)` = 0.6–0.9 second beat. Hold eye contact. No filler.
- No music under the hook. Minimal low bed from Beat 2. Music out entirely for the guarantee.
- Tone: calm, certain, slightly dry. The presenter is delivering an invoice audit, not hype.
- Numbers are spelled out the way they should be spoken. On-screen text shows the numerals.
- Hard cuts only. B-roll cutaways 2–4 seconds, always back to the presenter for the money lines.
- The hook must be performable deadpan. If the first sixty seconds sounds like an ad, re-take it.

## Beat 3 — The Mechanism: Three Layers (2:25–4:25)

**[SCENE: presenter. ON-SCREEN: "LAYER 1 — THE STRATEGY BRAIN" / "LAYER 2 — THE CREATIVE DEPARTMENT" / "LAYER 3 — THE TESTING MATRIX" appearing as each is named.]**

Here's what's actually inside. Three layers.

**[B-ROLL: metrics streaming into a dashboard; a Monday-morning report rendering as plain text.]**

Layer one: the strategy brain. GIZMO is your CMO and chief of staff in one. It ingests your numbers every hour — spend, hook rate, cost per acquisition, checkout data. It writes the media plan. It sets the budgets. It briefs the creative work. And every Monday morning it sends you the report a real CMO would: what won, what died, and what it's doing about it. In plain English.

**[B-ROLL: grid of fifty vertical videos rendering; UGC-style talking-head ad playing on a phone in-feed.]**

Layer two: the creative department. GIZMO plugs straight into our Content Engine — the production line that turns one idea into fifty finished video creatives a day. UGC-style talking heads. Hooks in the first second and a half. Captions burned in. B-roll cut over the slow middle.

And UGC-style creative is the whole game right now: it runs about four times the click-through at roughly half the cost per click of polished brand ads. Volume, plus native. That's 2026.

**[SCENE: presenter, tighter framing. ON-SCREEN: "100 variants. 1 variable per batch."]**

Layer three: the testing matrix. This is where statistical certainty replaces guessing.

Most agencies test three or four ads a month and call it optimization. GIZMO tests a hundred variants at a time — one variable isolated per batch. Hooks. Offers. Avatars. Openers.

Then it runs the ruthless loop no human team has the patience for. (pause)

Any variant under a twenty-five percent hook rate at forty-eight hours — dies. Winners get budgets raised twenty to thirty percent every three to four days. And every ten to fourteen days, fresh creative rolls in before fatigue touches your numbers.

**[ON-SCREEN: "Kill at <25% hook rate · Scale +20–30% every 3–4 days · Refresh every 10–14 days"]**

Your agency finds a winning ad by luck, a few times a year. A hundred-variant matrix finds one on schedule.

That's not better ads. That's different physics.

## Beat 5 — Objections: The Three Questions Smart Buyers Ask (5:40–7:40)

**[SCENE: presenter, seated, direct. ON-SCREEN: "1. Where does my data live?"]**

Now the three questions every smart buyer asks.

One: "Where does my data live?"

On your infrastructure, not ours. Every installation runs in its own isolated deployment, on a Tailscale-secured private network. Your ad accounts, your customer data, your API keys — none of it touches a shared system. None of it trains anyone else's models. None of it is visible to any other client. When we hand you the keys, they are actually your keys.

**[ON-SCREEN: "2. Will a machine embarrass my brand?" B-ROLL: approval queue on a phone; a creative getting rejected with one tap.]**

Two: "Will a machine embarrass my brand?"

No — because nothing ships without your sign-off. During install we encode your brand guardrails: banned claims, tone, compliance rules, visual rules. Every creative passes those gates, then lands in an approval queue on your phone. You start by approving everything. Most clients loosen the leash within a month — but that's your call, not ours.

**[ON-SCREEN: "3. Is AI creative good enough in 2026?"]**

Three: "Is AI creative actually good enough in 2026?"

Wrong question. The right question is: does the ad feel native to the feed? Because that's the only test the platform runs. UGC-style creative — the kind this system mass-produces — consistently beats polished brand ads on click-through and cost. Your customers have been scrolling past AI-assisted creative for two years. They don't care. The auction doesn't care. (pause) Only the numbers care.

**[SCENE: presenter leans in slightly. ON-SCREEN: "System + operator + accountability."]**

And one more — the honest one: "If AI tools can do this, why not just buy one myself?"

Because the market already ran that experiment. The self-serve AI ad tools all hit the same ceiling: software without an operator is a hobby. This isn't a tool you have to learn. It's a department we install, run, and stay accountable for. System, plus operator, plus accountability. That's the part you can't download.
